Why owners hire Omni for renos

The most expensive renovation is the one done twice.

Kitchens that earn the gut.

A kitchen renovation isn't about new cabinet boxes. It's about how light moves through the room at 7 AM and 7 PM, where the trash actually goes, and whether the island can fit four people pulling stools out at the same time. We design from how you cook on a random Tuesday, then layer the finishes on top.

Walls come down properly — or not at all.

The "open-concept" renovation that goes wrong is the one where someone took out a wall they shouldn't have and then a beam shows up too low or a column shows up in the middle of the kitchen. We engineer the structural opening before we draw the kitchen, so the beam tucks into the ceiling line and the room reads as if it was never closed in the first place.

Primary suites that actually feel new.

Most primary-suite renos fail at the same place — the closet still has the same footprint, the bath layout still pinches at the same corner, the door still hits the dresser. We move the walls that need moving, run the plumbing where it should have been, and rework the layout from how you actually use the room — not how it was framed in 1998.

Honest answers

The questions we get on every first call — and the answers, straight.

"Can we live in the house during the renovation?" +

For Foundation-band projects: usually yes — sealed dust-wall separation, single subcontractor entry, protected paths to bedrooms and a working bathroom. For Signature and whole-house renos: we recommend a short-term move-out for the demo + rough-in window (typically 6–10 weeks) and re-entry once the kitchen and at least one full bath are functional. We'll be honest about which phase is livable and which isn't.

"Should we just sell and buy something newer instead?" +

Run the numbers honestly. If your monthly payment is locked at a low rate and your property is on a lot you couldn't replicate today (oaks, view, school zone, half-acre+), renovating almost always wins on total cost over a 10-year horizon. If you're rate- flexible and the lot is replaceable, it's a closer call. We'll do the math with you on the first call — and if the answer is "sell," we'll tell you that too.

"What if you find something behind the wall?" +

Three categories of "behind-the-wall" surprises: structural (load paths, beams, headers), MEP (plumbing routing, electrical capacity, HVAC), and hazmat (asbestos, knob-and-tube, lead paint). We pre-empt the first two with selective demo during design, before we sign. Hazmat is the only true unknown — and Texas law requires testing before certain demo work, which we'll quote separately so there's no ambiguity in the contract.

"How much value does a renovation actually add?" +

Depends on the lot and the comps. In the Hill Country on a 1+ acre lot with mature trees, kitchen and primary-suite renovations typically recover 70–85% of cost at resale, with whole-house renos coming in closer to 65–80%. The honest answer: don't renovate to flip. Renovate because you plan to be in the house another 7–10+ years and want to enjoy it. The resale upside is the bonus, not the thesis.

"What's your warranty on a renovation?" +

One-year comprehensive on all workmanship in our scope. Two years on systems we install (electrical, plumbing, HVAC). Ten years on structural work (beams, headers, foundation modifications). The line you actually care about: 48-hour response window on every warranty ticket, written into the contract.

"Can you work with our existing designer or architect?" +

Yes — about 25% of our renovations come in with an outside designer already engaged. We'll do a free plan review first to flag anything missing (structural detail, MEP routing, code clearances) that would otherwise show up as a change order. If the plans are construction-ready, we go straight to pricing. If they need clean-up, we tell you what's missing and what it'll cost — before contract.
